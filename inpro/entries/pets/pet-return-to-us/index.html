Bringing pets back to the US

End-of-tour pet return is easier than import. USDA accepts most EU health certificates within 10 days. No quarantine. But microchip + rabies + flight arrangements still take 4–6 weeks of prep. Don't leave it to the last week.

What

Returning to the US with your pet is simpler than the import process was, but not trivial. The US requires proof of rabies vaccination (CDC rules shifted in 2024), identification by microchip, and proper flight arrangements. Most dogs and cats from Germany return with minimal friction.

Plan on 4-6 weeks of prep even though the paperwork window is 10 days.

The return pipeline

4-6 weeks out

  • Confirm pet’s ISO microchip still works; take pet to VTF or German vet for a scan
  • Rabies vaccine current (verify expiration; must be valid at time of arrival)
  • Choose return method: commercial cargo, Patriot Express, in-cabin, or pet relocation service
  • Flight booking with pet; book 4+ weeks out for cargo/PetSafe

2 weeks out

  • Identify USDA-accredited vet for final health certificate
  • Pet crate ready; IATA-compliant, acclimated
  • Pet food plan for transition; last few weeks of German food + start of US food

10 days out (the window)

  • German vet issues a new health certificate
  • Check CDC current requirements; rules changed in 2024 for dogs; confirm what applies to your pet’s return port

Flight day

  • Feed 4-6 hours before (not immediately before)
  • Bathroom break just before check-in
  • Arrive 2-3 hours early for cargo drop-off

US arrival

  • Pick up at destination; pets hold in cargo until you clear customs
  • CDC check if required (port of entry dependent)
  • Home quarantine; just 1-2 days to re-acclimate

CDC 2024 rule changes; what’s new

The CDC updated dog-import rules in 2024. Current requirements (verify closer to your return):

For dogs entering the US

  • Microchip (ISO 11784/11785 compatible); must match identification
  • At least 6 months old
  • Healthy at entry; no obvious illness
  • Rabies vaccination certificate required; CDC permit may be needed depending on port of entry
  • CDC Dog Import Form (new requirement since 2024)

For dogs from “rabies-free” countries (Germany qualifies)

  • Streamlined process; no USDA serology test required
  • Standard EU Health Certificate accepted at US ports of entry

For dogs from “high-rabies” countries

  • Different track; not applicable to Germany

Cats

  • Simpler than dogs; no CDC permit, standard health cert sufficient

Rules change; check current CDC guidance at cdc.gov/importation before travel.

Transportation options

Commercial airline (cargo)

  • United PetSafe / Delta Cargo / Lufthansa AnimalLounge; all have pet cargo services
  • Cost: $500-$2,000 one-way depending on size + route
  • Pet travels in climate-controlled cargo hold
  • Most reliable for medium/large pets

In-cabin (commercial)

  • Small pets (under 15-20 lb combined crate + pet) can fly in cabin with you
  • Cost: $100-300 one-way
  • Advantages: you’re together; less stress for pet
  • Disadvantages: crate must fit under seat; airline-specific rules

Patriot Express

  • DoD cargo pet shipment on military flights
  • Cost: cheaper than commercial cargo
  • Availability: schedule-dependent; book through gaining PCS coordinator
  • Best for medium-large dogs if schedule aligns

Pet relocation service

  • Companies: Across the Pond, PetRelocation, Starwood Pet Travel
  • Cost: $2,000-$5,000+ per pet
  • What they do: door-to-door including paperwork, flight booking, custom’s clearance, crate rental
  • Worth it for: large dogs, complex medical needs, owner unable to coordinate directly

At US arrival

Port of entry

Most common US ports for pets from Germany: Dulles (IAD), JFK, Philadelphia (PHL), Atlanta (ATL).

CDC clearance

Depending on port, your dog may need to clear CDC inspection. Some ports have CDC on-site 24/7; others require appointment. Confirm 48 hours before travel.

USDA inspection (if applicable)

Some cases require USDA vet inspection. Rare for pets returning to CDC-rabies-free-area US.

Paperwork in hand

Keep these ready at arrival:

  • Original health certificate (stamped)
  • Rabies vaccination certificate
  • Microchip paperwork
  • Your US ID (you’re responsible)

Preparing for the transition

2 weeks before flight

  • Acclimatize pet to crate
  • Introduce US-style food gradually (if switching brands)
  • Exercise + tire pet out day before travel

At the flight

  • Day-of tranquilizers: most airlines discourage sedation. Talk to vet.
  • Crate comfort: familiar blanket, one toy, no food or water during flight (ice water freezer-pack can melt slowly)
  • Frozen water bowl for long flights

After landing

  • Crate in the car: keep pet in crate until home for safety
  • Short walk first thing home; they need to pee after 10+ hours
  • Small feeding first day; not a full meal; gradually increase
  • Vet check within 2 weeks of arrival; monitor for travel stress

Documentation to save

For tax / insurance / customs records:

  • All veterinary invoices
  • Flight receipts
  • Crate receipt
  • Final health certificate (keep a copy; send USDA-version to home state vet)

Special cases

Pets over 14 years old / senior pets

  • May be more sensitive to cargo travel
  • Vet consultation recommended
  • Consider pet relocation service

Pets with medical conditions

  • Oxygen-requiring pets can fly but need special arrangements
  • Insulin-dependent: arrange storage + administration
  • Behavioral issues: may need sedation + monitoring

Breeds flagged in Germany

  • Bully breeds (Pit Bull type, Staffies): if your dog was legally owned in Hessen, the paperwork doesn’t disappear at the border. US reception is state-dependent; some states (CO, GA) have breed laws; others don’t.
  • Research your destination state’s breed laws before committing.

Multiple pets

  • Each pet: separate certificate, separate crate
  • Can fly on same flight but booked separately
  • Kennel requirements stricter for larger pets

German-side paperwork at departure

Some German administrators require:

  • Hundesteuer cancellation (Wiesbaden dog tax de-registration)
  • Notification to Stadt Wiesbaden of pet departure

For most cases, these are minor. Your Abmeldung (residency de-registration) is the main thing.

Gotchas

  • Don’t assume USDA from import is valid; a new health certificate is needed for return
  • 10-day window is strict; don’t miss it
  • Cargo bookings sell out; book 4-6 weeks ahead
  • Port-of-entry choice matters; some ports are easier than others (IAD vs. JFK differs)
  • In-cabin size limits vary by aircraft; check specific flight’s aircraft, not just the airline
  • State-of-arrival breed rules; bully breeds may have post-arrival state-level restrictions
  • Extreme weather embargoes: airlines refuse pets in cargo when temperatures are below 45°F or above 85°F at any point in the route. Plan around these seasons.
  • Pets left in Germany: if you can’t bring yours home, surrender via Tierheim (German animal shelter) is humane; they rehome aggressively. Don’t abandon.
