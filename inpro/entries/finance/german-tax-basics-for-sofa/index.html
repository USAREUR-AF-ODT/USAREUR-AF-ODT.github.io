German tax and SOFA: when you owe, when you don't

SOFA-status Active Duty and DA Civilians generally owe no German tax on US government salary. Spouses working on the German economy usually owe. German-sourced investment income can trigger German tax. US obligations continue regardless. Consult Legal when unsure.

What

The tax situation for a SOFA-status family in Wiesbaden hinges on three separate streams:

  1. US federal (and state) income tax; obligations continue regardless of where you live. File US taxes every year.
  2. German income tax (Einkommensteuer); SOFA status generally exempts Active Duty, Reserve, and DA Civilians from German tax on their US government salary. Other income is a different question.
  3. German tax on specific non-salary income; rental income, German investment returns, some business income can create German obligations regardless of SOFA status.

Many families misunderstand SOFA as “we owe no German tax on anything.” That is wrong for common scenarios: working spouses, rental properties in Germany, and some retirees.

If in doubt, talk to the USAG Legal Office tax team during tax season, or use a US-based preparer who specializes in military OCONUS returns.

Where

  • USAG Wiesbaden Legal Office; free tax-season consultation, Military VITA filing assistance
  • IRS Form 2350 and 4868; extensions if you need more time (you do; OCONUS is automatic 2-month extension)
  • German Finanzamt; the state tax office; issues tax IDs, receives German returns if you owe, audits
  • Military OneSource Financial and Tax Services; free phone and online consultation

When

  • US tax year runs Jan-Dec; returns due April 15 with automatic 2-month OCONUS extension to June 15; further extension to October 15 with Form 4868
  • German tax year also Jan-Dec; returns due July 31 (standard), October 31 with a tax advisor
  • Anmeldung triggers German tax registration; you get a Steueridentifikationsnummer (tax ID) within a few weeks of Anmeldung

Contact

  • USAG Wiesbaden Legal Office Tax Center; open late January through tax deadline each year; walk-in appointments for active duty, family members, retirees
  • IRS International Taxpayer Services; phone consultation for specific US questions
  • German tax advisor (Steuerberater); for any case where you owe or may owe German tax; fees typical €200-800 for an individual return
  • Military OneSource; free consultation 1-800-342-9647 or chat

US tax obligations; always on

Regardless of SOFA status, if you’re a US citizen or green-card holder, you file US taxes every year on your worldwide income. Key points:

  • Worldwide income is reported
  • Standard deduction or itemized; same options as stateside
  • Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE); Form 2555, can exclude up to ~$120k per person per year (2024 amount; updated annually) of foreign-earned income; does NOT apply to US military or DA Civilian salary (that’s US government-sourced income)
  • Foreign Tax Credit (FTC); Form 1116, credit against US tax for foreign income tax paid; applies when a spouse pays German tax on German-earned income
  • Combat Zone Tax Exclusion (CZTE); applies to military pay earned in designated zones; not Wiesbaden base pay, but applies to forward deployments
  • State tax; depends on your state of legal residence (SLR); many military families change SLR to a no-income-tax state (FL, TX, WA, NV) pre-PCS to simplify
  • Filing deadlines: April 15 regular, June 15 OCONUS automatic, October 15 with extension

Don’t skip US returns because you “live overseas.” The IRS knows where you are; DFAS reports your US salary.

German tax; when SOFA exempts and when it doesn’t

US government salary; generally exempt

  • Active Duty military pay; generally not taxed by Germany under the NATO SOFA
  • DA Civilian salary (if you’re command-sponsored, SOFA-stamped); generally not taxed by Germany
  • NAF employee salary; generally SOFA-exempt
  • Military retirement pay; complicated; depends on your specific situation and treaty interpretation

Income that IS often taxed by Germany

  • Working spouse earning on German economy; if your spouse works for a German company (not as a DA Civilian or SOFA-covered contractor), Germany generally taxes that salary. Spouse needs a German tax ID, possibly a Lohnsteuerkarte (wage tax card).
  • Self-employment or freelance income from German clients; taxable in Germany
  • Rental property in Germany; owner earns income from a German asset; Germany taxes the rental income
  • German investment income; dividends, interest from German brokerage accounts; Germany applies Abgeltungsteuer (withholding tax, ~25%)
  • Home business run from Germany serving non-US clients; often taxable in Germany
  • Crypto and German exchanges; Germany taxes some crypto gains; rules evolving

The tie-breaker; SOFA document vs. dual status

A non-working SOFA-status family member generally doesn’t need a German tax ID. The moment a family member takes German-economy employment, they cross into dual-status territory:

  • German tax on German-earned income
  • US tax on US-earned income
  • US tax on German-earned income (reported on US return; FTC or FEIE may apply)

Double taxation is avoided through the US-Germany Tax Treaty and through the FTC/FEIE mechanisms, but you may need to file in both countries.

Working-spouse specifics

The most common “German tax surprise” scenario:

  • Spouse takes a part-time job at a Wiesbaden café, a language school, or a German company
  • Employer issues Lohnsteuerbescheinigung (annual wage statement)
  • Germany has withheld Lohnsteuer (wage tax) and Solidaritätszuschlag (solidarity surcharge) throughout the year
  • Spouse files German tax return to reconcile; may get refund or owe more
  • US return: spouse’s German wages reported; either FEIE excludes them (up to the cap) or FTC credits the German tax paid

If your spouse plans to work on the German economy:

  • Apply for Steueridentifikationsnummer (tax ID) via Anmeldung; or separately at the Finanzamt
  • Get a Lohnsteuerklasse (wage tax class) assignment; depends on family status
  • Arrange a German tax advisor for year-end filing; first year costs €400-800 typical

Rental property back in the US

Generally not taxed by Germany (US-sourced income). Report on US return as normal. Depreciation, deductions, etc. follow US rules. Keep good records; US audit probability rises modestly for OCONUS filers.

Rental property in Germany

Taxed by Germany. You file a German Einkommensteuer return with rental income on Anlage V. Costs €500+ for a tax advisor. Also reported on US return (worldwide income), with FTC for German tax paid.

Investment accounts; PFIC trap

Passive Foreign Investment Companies (PFICs) are a US tax trap. Most non-US mutual funds and ETFs held in German brokerage accounts are PFICs from the US perspective. PFIC reporting is onerous and expensive; IRS Form 8621 per fund, per year.

Practical advice: avoid buying European-domiciled mutual funds and ETFs in a German brokerage account. Keep your long-term investments in US-domiciled accounts (Vanguard, Fidelity, Schwab) at a US address. If you already have European funds, consult a US tax specialist before moving them.

State of legal residence (SLR)

  • Soldiers on Active Duty can maintain SLR independent of physical location under SCRA (Servicemembers Civil Relief Act)
  • Spouses under MSRRA (Military Spouse Residency Relief Act) can maintain the soldier’s SLR for tax purposes, with conditions
  • Changing SLR to a no-income-tax state pre-PCS saves state tax for the duration of the tour
  • Don’t change SLR casually; affects voting, vehicle registration, driver’s license, and legal disputes

Common military-friendly SLRs: Florida, Texas, Washington, Nevada, Wyoming, South Dakota, Alaska, Tennessee, New Hampshire (interest/dividend tax), Virginia (some military exemptions).

Military VITA at the USAG Legal Office

Each year, the Legal Office runs a Volunteer Income Tax Assistance (VITA) program:

  • Free, IRS-certified volunteers
  • OCONUS-aware; they handle typical military tax situations (combat pay, foreign addresses, deployment-year filings)
  • Appointment-based; book early January; peak demand mid-February through April
  • Software used: TaxSlayer or similar; e-files returns
  • Limitations: VITA handles straightforward returns; rental properties, self-employment, complex investments may exceed scope; ask at appointment

For complex returns (working spouse with German income, rental property, business income), use a paid tax preparer who specializes in military expats. Worth the fee.

Gotchas

  • “SOFA means no German tax” is wrong when your spouse works on the German economy. Budget for spouse-side German tax planning.
  • US tax filing doesn’t stop because you’re overseas. File every year.
  • State tax can follow you if your SLR is a state with tax (NY, CA, etc.). Establishing FL/TX/WA/NV before PCS is common.
  • FEIE does NOT apply to military or DA Civilian pay. That income is US government-sourced, not foreign-earned.
  • PFIC rules make European mutual funds a US tax nightmare. Avoid them in German brokerage accounts.
  • German inheritance; if you inherit property in Germany during your tour, German inheritance tax may apply. Consult Legal immediately.
  • Failure to file German return when required; penalties, interest, and Finanzamt correspondence that’s hard to resolve after you PCS. Don’t skip.
  • Anmeldung gives you a Steueridentifikationsnummer; this doesn’t mean you owe German tax on your military pay; it means Germany has issued you an ID number. Don’t panic.
  • Joint US filing with non-US-citizen spouse; election required; has implications. Legal Office can walk through.
  • Foreign bank account reporting (FBAR); any US person with aggregate foreign account balances over $10,000 at any time during the year must file FinCEN Form 114 (FBAR). Separate from your tax return; deadline is April 15 with automatic extension.
  • FATCA (Form 8938); additional foreign-financial-asset reporting threshold; applies to some families.
  • Bringing large sums to Germany for a home purchase; declarable; ask Legal about customs and IRS implications.
  • Crypto; German and US rules both apply; record-keeping matters.
  • Tax software limitations; TurboTax handles most OCONUS military situations; check it covers your situation (foreign address, military pay, deployment months) before paying.
  • Keep 7+ years of returns and supporting documents. US audit lookback for foreign-income issues extends.

Source notes

Tax structure for SOFA families drawn from IRS international taxpayer guidance, the German Federal Ministry of Finance, and Military OneSource tax resources (verified 2026-04-20). Specific rules and rates change annually; treat this entry as orientation, not advice. Individual situations vary widely; use the USAG Legal Office VITA program or a military-expat tax preparer for filing.
