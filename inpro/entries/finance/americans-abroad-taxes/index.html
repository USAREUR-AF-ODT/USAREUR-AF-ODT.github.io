US taxes while stationed overseas: FBAR, FEIE, state residency

You still file US federal taxes from Germany. SOFA-status shields you from German income tax, but FBAR reports any German bank account over $10K. Your state of legal residence (SLR) decides whether you owe state tax. File via VITA at Legal Office (free, Jan–Apr).

What

You’re stationed in Germany, paid in USD, probably living off-post with a German bank account and a German landlord. Your US tax obligations don’t pause. This page covers the surprises.

Three things to understand:

  • US federal taxes: still due. File every year from Germany.
  • State taxes: depends on your State of Legal Residence (SLR)
  • FBAR (foreign account reporting): required if German account balance exceeds $10K at any point in the year

SOFA shields you from German income tax on your DoD-source salary. It does NOT shield you from US federal tax.

US federal taxes while abroad

You still file

  • Same April 15 deadline (+2 month automatic extension for overseas, so June 15)
  • Same forms (1040 + applicable schedules)
  • Same income rules

What changes

  • Residence-abroad automatic extension: June 15 instead of April 15
  • Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE): $120,000+/year of earned income excluded from federal tax IF you qualify. Active Duty Soldiers usually don’t qualify (they’re US government employees on US wages). Spouses with German or remote US jobs might.
  • Foreign Tax Credit (FTC): if you pay German income tax (unusual as SOFA-shielded), you claim credit
  • German housing allowance (OHA): not taxable at US federal level either; it’s a housing allowance, not wages

Active Duty Soldier specifically

  • Your wages: taxable federally as normal
  • OHA: not taxable (section 107 housing allowance for Active Duty)
  • COLA: not taxable
  • TLA: not taxable
  • VAT savings: not taxable (not reportable income)
  • State tax: depends on SLR (see below)

DA Civilian specifically

  • Your wages: taxable federally
  • OHA: not taxable (LQA = Living Quarters Allowance)
  • Post Allowance (your COLA equivalent): not taxable
  • State tax: depends on SLR

Contractor specifically

  • Your wages: fully taxable federally (generally not SOFA-shielded on US wages unless specific contract structure)
  • Talk to a tax advisor who understands OCONUS contractor compensation

State of Legal Residence (SLR)

This is where it gets nuanced. SLR determines which US state considers you a resident for tax purposes. SLR is distinct from:

  • Home of Record (HoR): where you entered military service
  • Duty station: Wiesbaden (doesn’t make you a state resident)
  • Where you own a house / rent: may or may not match SLR

States with NO income tax

If your SLR is one of these, you owe zero state tax: Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire (interest/dividends only until 2027), South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington, Wyoming

Active Duty Soldiers can change SLR while stationed, typically through voter registration + driver’s license + bank account in the new state.

States that exempt Active Duty abroad

Many states exempt Active Duty military stationed out-of-state from state tax while deployed/stationed elsewhere. Rules vary by state. Check your specific state’s military-resident rules at your state’s revenue site.

How to change SLR

You can change SLR by establishing intent + residency ties in the new state:

  1. Register to vote in the new state
  2. Obtain driver’s license in the new state
  3. Open bank account in the new state
  4. Change mailing address
  5. File SLR form (military only): DD Form 2058

Do this before PCS or during a re-leave visit stateside. Can’t change SLR from Germany without a stateside visit.

State tax credits if you paid another state

If you moved mid-year, both states may claim tax. Dual-residency credit typically reconciles.

FBAR; the surprise most people miss

FBAR: Report of Foreign Bank and Financial Accounts. Filed with FinCEN (not IRS), annually, by April 15 (auto-extended to Oct 15 in most cases).

Who files

Anyone with foreign financial accounts aggregating over $10,000 at ANY point during the year. Including:

  • German checking + savings accounts (combined if you have multiple)
  • German brokerage accounts
  • Business accounts if you have signing authority
  • Cryptocurrency held on foreign exchanges

What triggers it

  • Moving €20K to your German bank for rent + deposits? Yes.
  • Sitting at €5K for most of the year but peaking at $10K+ during a transfer? Yes, you file.
  • Sparkasse, N26, Revolut, or any German-registered account? Yes, even online-only.

How to file

  • Online at FinCEN’s BSA E-Filing system (bsaefiling.fincen.treas.gov)
  • Free to file
  • Takes 15-30 minutes
  • Report highest balance for each account during the year
  • Separate from your 1040

Penalties for not filing

  • Civil: up to $14,000 per account per year
  • Criminal (willful): up to $250,000 + 5 years prison
  • Non-willful failure: $500-$1,000 per account (still significant)

Don’t skip this. Many PCS Soldiers don’t know about FBAR. Plan on filing it every year you have a German bank account.

Form 8938 (FATCA); another foreign asset report

If your foreign assets exceed certain thresholds ($200K unmarried OCONUS, $400K married), you ALSO file Form 8938 with your 1040. Separate from FBAR; covers broader assets.

German tax obligations

Generally exempt

SOFA-status US military and DA Civilians are exempt from German income tax on DoD-source wages.

When you pay German tax

  • Spouse working on German economy: German income tax on German wages
  • Freelancer/self-employed: German tax applies
  • Rental property in Germany: income tax on rental income
  • German investment income: taxable

German tax return (Steuererklärung)

Most SOFA personnel don’t file one. Spouses with German income do. Deadline: May 31 of following year (or October with a tax advisor).

VITA; the free military tax prep

Volunteer Income Tax Assistance (VITA) runs at the Wiesbaden Legal Office every tax season (January-April). Trained volunteers prepare federal + state returns for free.

What they cover

  • 1040 + schedules
  • State returns (most states)
  • OCONUS-specific issues (OHA exclusion, PCS travel)

What they don’t

  • Self-employment / 1099 work (consult paid preparer)
  • Real estate rental
  • Complex investments (K-1s, etc.)
  • FBAR (you file yourself; they don’t do foreign account reporting)

Book early

Appointments fill January through April. Book in January if possible.

Tax software + alternatives

Free file

  • IRS Free File (if AGI under $79K)
  • MilTax (free for Active Duty + Family members)

Paid

  • TurboTax; standard choice; handles overseas addresses fine
  • H&R Block Online; also works
  • H&R Block retail; rare in Wiesbaden; US visits only

Tax pro

If you have complex situations (spouse’s German income, rental property, investment income), a CPA specializing in expat military is worth it; $300-$800 per return.

PCS year specifically

Your PCS year has overlapping considerations:

Moving expenses

  • Active Duty: mostly non-taxable (military move tax exclusion)
  • DA Civilian: partially non-taxable (certain reimbursements still excluded)

State tax split

  • Prorate if you changed SLR
  • Part-year returns possible

Travel voucher

  • Not taxable income
  • Reported on LES for tracking

Keep records

For each tax year, save:

  • Final LES (December)
  • All W-2s
  • All 1099s (spouse’s jobs, bank interest)
  • FBAR filing confirmation
  • Bank statements from German accounts (December balance)
  • Rent receipts (documentation for OHA if audited)

Keep 7 years; audit window is 3 but extended for significant understatements.

Gotchas

  • FBAR is easy to forget; set a calendar reminder for April each year
  • State tax blind spot: if you didn’t change SLR before PCS, you may still owe Virginia / California / NY tax (whichever was your SLR)
  • Spouse’s US remote-work state tax: some states aggressively pursue spouses who “work remotely from abroad”; research your state’s rule
  • Cryptocurrency on foreign exchanges: FBAR-reportable if aggregate >$10K
  • Joint filing with non-US spouse: election required on first joint return after arrival
  • Children born in Germany: US citizens if sponsor is; file Form 8812 and claim dependent credit normally
  • State residency while deployed: the Servicemembers Civil Relief Act protects Active Duty from state tax complications in their non-resident state. Don’t let “but you live in Germany” fool a state into taxing you as a non-resident.
  • Amending prior returns: Form 1040-X (US) + similar state forms. Three-year window.
  • Don’t wait until April 14; some foreign-asset rules are complex; start in February.

Source notes

Synthesized from IRS International Taxpayers guidance, IRS FEIE rules, FinCEN FBAR requirements, and MilitaryOneSource Wiesbaden financial guidance. Tax law updates annually; always verify current rules at IRS.gov before filing. Consult a CPA or VITA volunteer if your situation is complex.

Enrollment requires 30+ consecutive days in a CZTE zone. Deposit up to your monthly unallotted base pay. Withdraw within 90 days of returning. Few products match this return.
