Internet and mobile phone setup

Deutsche Telekom or 1&1 for home internet; O2, Vodafone, or a prepaid SIM for mobile. Contracts run 24 months and auto-renew: beware. For short-term, a prepaid SIM costs €10–€30/month with good coverage.

What

Two separate decisions: home internet (for off-post housing) and mobile phone. On-post quarters usually include internet in a bundle; off-post housing doesn’t.

Germany has four major mobile carriers and three dominant home-internet providers. All of them work. The differences are in contract length, price tier, and customer service.

Mobile phone

Quick answer: get a prepaid SIM for arrival

Before you commit to a 24-month contract, grab a prepaid SIM at any grocery store, dm, or Rossmann. Prepaid options:

  • Aldi Talk (on Telefónica/O2 network): €10 top-up lasts ~1 month with basic use
  • Congstar (on Telekom network): slightly pricier, better coverage in rural areas
  • Lidl Mobile (on Vodafone network): basic plan
  • freenet / winSIM / sipgate; smaller resellers, often bundled deals

Prepaid SIMs are activated with a passport. You’ll get a German phone number immediately. You can keep it or switch to contract later.

Contracts (long-term, better rates)

After Anmeldung, you can sign a contract with:

  • Deutsche Telekom (T-Mobile); best coverage, highest price
  • Vodafone; good coverage, mid-tier
  • O2 / Telefónica; cheaper, patchy rural coverage

Contract typical:

  • 12 or 24 months (24 standard; 12 costs more)
  • €15-€40/month depending on data
  • Unlimited calls + SMS to Germany standard
  • Data allowances 3 GB to unlimited
  • EU roaming included (you can use your German SIM in France, Italy, etc. without extra fees)

Gotchas

  • Contracts auto-renew unless you cancel 3 months before expiry. Set a calendar reminder.
  • PCS-out; Germany’s Sonderkündigungsrecht (special termination right) allows contract cancellation with proof you’re leaving the country. Present your PCS orders at the carrier store 3-4 weeks before departure.
  • SIM unlock; new German phones are usually unlocked; older US phones may be locked to a US carrier. Check with your carrier before leaving CONUS.
  • Do you need a German number? If you’re keeping your US number on WhatsApp/iMessage for family, a prepaid SIM is fine. A German number matters for German-side services (delivery, appointments, German bank OTP codes).

Home internet

Providers

  • Deutsche Telekom; flagship. Largest network, best support, most expensive.
  • 1&1; second-largest. Cheaper than Telekom; similar coverage.
  • Vodafone Kabel; cable-based (not DSL). Often faster, sometimes cheaper.
  • O2 Home; newer, cheaper, less reliable in some areas.
  • Local Stadtwerke ISPs (Stadtwerke Wiesbaden); municipal broadband, sometimes best value in specific neighborhoods.

What you pick

  • Speed tier: 100 Mbps is standard; 250-1,000 Mbps available in most of Wiesbaden. Gigabit isn’t needed for most families.
  • Contract length: 24 months standard. Switching mid-contract costs.
  • Bundle: some providers bundle TV or landline; you probably don’t need either if you’re US military.

Expected monthly:

  • 100 Mbps: €30-€40
  • 250 Mbps: €40-€50
  • 1,000 Mbps: €60-€80

Setup timeline

  • Contract signup: immediate
  • Technician visit: 1-4 weeks after signup for DSL; cable is usually faster
  • Hardware (router): mailed or delivered at visit
  • You pay from signup date, not from connection date; ask for “Anschlusstermin” (connection date) in writing before signing

Fritz!Box

The FRITZ!Box is the dominant German router. Pre-configured with most ISPs. Easy to use, widely documented in English online. Keep the one your ISP provides; they often bill extra if you return it early.

Rundfunkbeitrag isn’t a cable TV tax

Despite sounding like a TV fee, Rundfunkbeitrag (€18.36/month) is paid regardless of whether you have TV; it’s the public broadcasting tax. You pay it once per household, off-post, whether you watch or not. See the Legal / Residency entries for more.

International calling from Germany

From Germany to the US:

  • Dial 00 1 + area code + number (e.g., 00 1 555 123 4567 for (555) 123-4567)
  • WhatsApp / FaceTime / iMessage over Wi-Fi always free

From the US to Germany:

  • Dial 011 49 + German number without the leading 0

Work-from-home / tethering tips

If you’ll work remotely or take video calls from home:

  • 50 Mbps download / 10 Mbps upload is the minimum for reliable video
  • Test your home Wi-Fi in the room you’ll use before a big call
  • Tethering from a mobile SIM works for emergencies; don’t rely on it long-term

Gotchas

  • Contract termination requires written notice (email is often fine now). Call is not enough.
  • Early termination fees can be 1-3 months of service cost. PCS orders usually waive this.
  • Router ownership; at contract end, you may owe money if you don’t return the ISP-provided router.
  • Internet outage; German ISPs aren’t as responsive as US ones. A weekend outage may not be fixed until Monday. Keep a prepaid SIM with data as backup.
  • eSIM support is inconsistent across German carriers. Check before relying on it.
  • TV streaming services (Netflix, Disney+) work on Wi-Fi here, but catalog is European. If you want US Netflix, you need a US VPN.
  • Airport Wi-Fi at FRA is free but slow. Your German SIM with EU roaming works everywhere.
  • Phone number portability; bringing your old German number to a new carrier takes 3-7 days. Plan around it if you switch mid-tour.

Source notes

Carrier landscape and contract norms are general community knowledge for Germany; cross-referenced with the 2024 Welcome Packet’s phone-setup section. Prices and contract terms change frequently; confirm current rates at the specific carrier’s website or a retail store before signing.
