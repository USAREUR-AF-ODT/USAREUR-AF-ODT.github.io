German survival basics: the 20 things that make Germany easier

Sundays are closed. Pfand is recycling. You greet when you enter a small shop. Recycling has 4 bins. Bakeries are cash-only more than you'd think. Nobody's being rude, it's just how it works.

Last verified 2026-04-16 6 min read

What

The cultural stuff that welcome briefings gloss over. None of it is hard once you know the rule; all of it is awkward when you don’t.

The rules

1. Sundays are closed

No grocery stores, no pharmacies, no Amazon delivery, no DIY store. Gas stations and the bakeries that open mornings (Sonntagsbrötchen tradition) are the main exceptions. Restaurants, museums, and gyms are open; retail isn’t. Plan grocery shopping for Saturday.

2. Cash is still king at small businesses

Bakeries, cafés, small restaurants, market stalls, taxi drivers; many don’t take cards. Keep €50-€100 cash on you. Larger stores and most restaurants do take cards; small ones are inconsistent.

3. Greet when you enter a small shop

“Guten Morgen” or “Hallo” when you walk into a bakery, butcher, or shop smaller than a supermarket. “Tschüss” or “Auf Wiedersehen” when you leave. Not greeting registers as cold. The clerk returns the greeting; it’s part of the transaction.

4. Recycling is serious business

Four-bin system:

  • Yellow bag / Gelber Sack; plastic, cans, composite packaging (Tetra Paks)
  • Paper / Papier (blue); paper, cardboard
  • Bio (brown); food waste, compostables
  • Residual / Restmüll (grey/black); everything that isn’t the above

Plus Pfand (deposit-return) for bottles; see below. Get it wrong and your neighbors will tell you. Neighborhood waste calendars are distributed annually; on-post housing has them in the welcome packet.

5. Pfand (bottle deposits)

Plastic bottles and glass bottles carry a deposit (€0.08; €0.25 per bottle) built into the purchase price. Return empty bottles to any grocery store that sells them, get a receipt, cash it at checkout (or apply it to your groceries). Throwing a Pfand bottle in the trash = literal money wasted.

6. Ruhezeit; quiet hours

German federal law: Sunday all day and 22:00-06:00 weekdays are legally mandated quiet hours. No lawnmowers, no loud music, no construction. Neighbors will call the Ordnungsamt (public order office) if you violate. The on-post version is slightly more relaxed; off-post, take it seriously.

7. Don’t walk in bike lanes

Bike lanes are separate from pedestrian paths and often painted red. Walking on one gets you swept by a cyclist going 25 km/h. Check the pavement. When in doubt, stay right and look both ways before crossing.

8. Jaywalking is taboo

Germans wait at red pedestrian lights even if the street is empty. Cross on red and you’ll catch stares; especially with kids present. It’s a community norm, not traffic law enforcement.

9. Tipping is 5-10%, rounded

At a restaurant, tell the waiter the total you want to pay including tip; they make change to that amount. Don’t leave cash on the table; hand it to the waiter. “Stimmt so” means “keep the change.”

Examples:

  • Bill €43.50, you want to tip ~10%: say “47 Euro, bitte.”
  • Bill €18.50: “20 Euro, bitte” works.

10. Cold drinks

Germans prefer drinks at room temperature, especially water and beer. If you want ice water, ask for “Wasser mit Eis, bitte”; and maybe a knowing look. Most Americans never adjust; that’s fine.

11. Tap water vs. bottled

German tap water is excellent and free. Restaurants serve bottled (still = stilles Wasser, sparkling = Wasser mit Kohlensäure or Sprudel); and charge for it. Asking for tap water at a restaurant (Leitungswasser) is unusual but possible.

12. Your privacy is theirs

Germans respect privacy, both yours and theirs. Don’t photograph people (especially kids) without consent. Don’t ask personal questions too early (income, politics, religion). Small talk is less expected than in the US; a direct “Hallo” and the transaction is fine.

13. Work-life separation is real

German coworkers don’t text at 9pm. Weekends are family time. Don’t take a non-response as rude; it’s healthy. Urgent military issues obviously still move; but the line between work and life is sharper.

14. Names and titles

“Herr Müller” and “Frau Schmidt” in formal contexts. First names come after the person invites it. Doctors and academics are often “Herr Doktor” or “Frau Professor.” Bureaucrats appreciate the title.

15. The train and the bus run on time

If the schedule says 07:34, the train arrives at 07:34. It may depart at 07:35. Show up 5 minutes early, not 5 minutes late. Germans standing on a platform at 07:33 are staring at the clock; join them.

16. Bakeries open early, close early

Small neighborhood bakeries open 06:00-07:00 and close by 18:00. Large chains (Back-Werk, Kamps) may stay open later. The fresh bread you want is gone by 10:00 on Saturday.

17. Bread is cultural heritage

German bread is a Unesco-recognized cultural artifact. There are 3,200+ official varieties. Try more than just Brötchen. Pumpernickel, Roggen, Vollkorn, Dinkelbrot, Sonnenblumenbrot; they’re all different. Find your local favorite.

18. Beer and wine culture

Every region has its own beer styles. Wiesbaden is in the Rheingau; Riesling country. Learn to order by style (halbtrocken = off-dry, trocken = dry, feinherb = medium-dry). Beer comes in specific glass shapes for specific beers; the waiter brings the right glass.

19. Apotheke (pharmacy)

German pharmacies are separate from grocery stores. They sell OTC meds, prescriptions, some cosmetics. They’re closed Sundays (except one “Notdienst” pharmacy per night on rotation; check the Apotheke window for the current weekly schedule).

20. Emergencies and civil courage

Germans have a legal obligation of Zivilcourage; helping others in distress. If you see an accident, Germans will stop to help. In emergencies, shout “Hilfe!” (help) to alert others. The norm is active intervention, not walking past.

Bonus: language survival kit

You don’t need fluent German to live here. These phrases cover 80% of daily interactions:

  • Guten Morgen / Tag / Abend; good morning/day/evening
  • Hallo / Tschüss; hi / bye (informal)
  • Bitte / Danke; please / thank you
  • Entschuldigung; excuse me / sorry
  • Sprechen Sie Englisch?; do you speak English?
  • Ich verstehe nicht; I don’t understand
  • Wie viel kostet das?; how much does it cost?
  • Die Rechnung, bitte; the check, please
  • Zum Mitnehmen; to go (takeaway)
  • Stimmt so; keep the change
  • Nur kurz eine Frage; just a quick question

Most service staff in Wiesbaden speak some English. Leading with a German phrase (“Hallo, sprechen Sie Englisch?”) gets warmer responses than opening in English.

German classes

  • ACS Relocation Readiness offers free basic German classes
  • Volkshochschule Wiesbaden (VHS); the city’s adult education school, excellent German courses at every level, €100-€300 per semester
  • Goethe Institute Frankfurt; the gold-standard intensive German school; 1-hour train; more expensive

Gotchas

  • The German directness is not rudeness. “This is wrong” is information, not an insult. Don’t take feedback personally.
  • Small talk is less common but not absent. Weather, kids, pets; fine topics. Politics, salary, the war; not at first meeting.
  • “Gemütlich”; a German word meaning warm, cozy, inviting. Aim for gemütlich spaces in your home and in your social life; it’s how Germans describe the good times.
  • Nothing will open on a federal holiday; double-check the calendar before planning a Saturday errand. Hessen holidays include Good Friday, Easter Monday, Whit Monday, Corpus Christi.
  • Don’t jaywalk in front of a child. Parents get actively upset. It’s a cultural norm enforced via social pressure.
  • German birthdays: birthday person brings cake / treats to work, not the other way around. Congratulating someone before their actual birthday is considered bad luck.

Source notes

Host Nation Orientation section of the 2024 Welcome Packet is the official brief; this page’s voice and specifics come from community knowledge validated against Stadt Wiesbaden’s English-language living pages. Rules around Ruhezeit and recycling are enforced by law; cultural rules are enforced by social norm.
